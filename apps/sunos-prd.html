Purpose

To align on what SunOS is, which surfaces ship first, who owns what, and how requirements stay the source of truth across design, engineering, and automation.

Definition

What is SunOS.

SunOS is a project operating system. It centralizes project folders, requirements, references, roadmaps, feeds, and workflows so that every product Sunspell launches can be specified, designed, and shipped from a single source of truth.

The core idea

Each project — for example Sunspoke — lives inside a folder with structured source-of-truth files: design.md, tech.md, brand.md, product.md, launch.md, roadmap.md, and potentially website, deck, or prototype-related files. These files act as the core requirements layer for building products.

What SunOS is not

  • Not a customer-facing product (yet).
  • Not a replacement for Slack Canvas or Notion as a chat tool — it replaces them as the requirements substrate.
  • Not Figma. Figma stays for conceptual direction; production design moves to Paper.
Problem

Why this exists.

The team is launching multiple products quickly and the requirements that drive each one are scattered across tools, transcripts, screenshots, and personal local files. Without a centralized source of truth, design, engineering, and automation cannot stay in sync.

  • No single requirements substrate. PRDs live in Slack Canvas, Notion, Figma, and local files; nothing is canonical.
  • Meetings produce signal that gets lost. Transcripts capture decisions but do not propagate into the requirement files that designers and agents read.
  • Saves and references are scattered. Instagram saves, links, competitor references, brand references, tools, and APIs live across personal accounts; they cannot inform a project's requirements.
  • Roadmaps and tasks are disconnected from requirements. Weekly tasks are generated by hand instead of read out of the project's source of truth.
  • Automation cannot be trusted yet. Without strong requirements, Paper / Claude / agents produce work that has to be re-explained and re-corrected every cycle.
Vision

North star.

SunOS becomes the engine the team uses to launch every Sunspell product. Requirements live in MD files; design, prototypes, roadmaps, weekly tasks, and missions are derived from those files. Eventually the same engine can be licensed externally or used as proof of proprietary infrastructure when fundraising.

  1. Internal first. SunOS is the team's internal operating system. Its first job is to ship Sunspoke faster and more coherently.
  2. Source-of-truth driven. Every artifact downstream — wireframes, prototypes, roadmaps, tasks, missions — traces back to MD files in a project folder.
  3. Optional external paths later. Once internal value is proven, SunOS may support consulting engagements, license to agencies, or anchor a fundraising story for Sunspell / Sunspoke.

Principles

Rules SunOS designs against.

Non-negotiables that come out of the meeting and the standing rules in CLAUDE.md.

  • Internal first. Build for the three-person team before anyone outside. Do not simplify for hypothetical customers.
  • Requirements are the source of truth. MD files drive design, prototypes, roadmap generation, and automation. If a downstream artifact disagrees with the MD, the MD wins.
  • Approval before write. Every change to a requirement file is proposed in Feed and approved before it commits. Bulk-approve is fine; granular diff review is required for advanced cases.
  • One project at a time. Only Sunspoke until the OS proves itself. No multi-project sprawl.
  • Own the system. The MD files live in something the team owns — not Slack Canvas, not Notion. Build the centralized substrate, even if it means an internal repo / editor / MCP server.
  • Paper is production. Figma stays light, for conceptual direction. Paper is where wireframes, prototypes, and (eventually) polished design get made.

Model

MD files drive everything.

The MD files are not documentation. They are the substrate. Design, prototypes, roadmap generation, weekly tasks, and missions all read from them.

Downstream artifacts derived from the MD files

  • Wireframes in Paper (driven by design.md + product.md).
  • Prototypes (greenlit wireframes promoted to clickable prototypes).
  • PRD decks (this file pattern).
  • Roadmap and weekly tasks in Sunline (driven by roadmap.md).
  • Missions in Sunrun (driven by launch.md, tech.md, etc.).
  • Marketing site / cover imagery (driven by brand.md).

Why this matters

Strong requirements are essential because Paper can produce strong wireframes, prototypes, and possibly polished designs if the source of truth is clear enough. Weak requirements force re-briefing every cycle.

Updates

How requirements change.

Requirement updates can come from three places. All three route through Feed and require approval before they commit.

1 · Manual Claude commits

A team member edits the MD files directly through Claude or another client. Changes appear in Feed as a proposed commit.

2 · Meeting transcripts

Transcripts (e.g. this meeting, captured via GPT 5.5 Thinking + Assembly AI) are ingested. The system proposes requirement updates derived from the transcript.

3 · Sunpool saves / references

A save associated with a project (e.g. a Sunspoke competitor reference) can trigger a proposed update to that project's MD files.

Surface · Feed

Feed — activity & approval.

Feed is where transcripts, saves, Claude commits, and other changes appear. It proposes requirement updates and lets the team approve all changes or review granular diffs before committing.

What flows into Feed

  • Manual Claude commits to MD files.
  • Meeting transcripts ready for ingestion.
  • Proposed requirement changes derived from transcripts or saves.
  • Sunpool save events associated with a project.

Approval controls (Jasmine to spec)

  • Bulk approve — accept a whole batch of changes at once.
  • Advanced granular approval — review individual changes one at a time.
  • Diff review — see the before / after of any proposed change.
  • Revert / version history — roll back any approved change.
  • Transcript-ready notifications — let the team know when a transcript is parseable into proposals.
Surface · Sunpool

Sunpool — memory & references.

The memory / reference layer, similar to a Pinterest-style library. Saves from Instagram, links, competitors, brand references, tools, APIs, or ideas can be added to Sunpool and associated with projects. These saves can trigger suggested updates to requirement files.

Save types

  • Instagram saves.
  • Links.
  • Competitor references.
  • Brand references.
  • Tools.
  • APIs.
  • Ideas.

Behaviors

  • Saves can be associated with a project (e.g. Sunspoke).
  • An associated save can trigger a Feed proposal to update that project's MD files.
  • The team should be able to text into the Sunpool database (via Michael's infrastructure).

Owner

Michael — Sunpool is the highest immediate technical priority.

Surface · Sunline

Sunline — projects & tasks.

The project / task management layer. Reads roadmaps and requirements, generates weekly recommended tasks, and lets the team accept or reject them. Eventually functions like a smarter task tracker connected to the project source of truth.

Behaviors

  • Reads roadmap.md (and other MD files) to understand the project's plan.
  • Generates weekly recommended tasks.
  • Lets the team accept or reject each recommendation.
  • Functions like a task tracker — but the source of truth is the MD files, not the task list.

Dependencies

Sunline depends on the source-of-truth substrate (MD files) being centralized and readable. It is lower priority than Sunpool because Sunpool is unblocking other work.

Surface · Sunrun

Sunrun — workflow automation.

Sunrun does not need its own main interface. It lives inside projects, Sunpool, and Sunline as executable missions or workflows.

Mission examples

  • Finding partnerships.
  • Generating marketing activation ideas.
  • Contacting vendors.
  • Creating JDs (job descriptions).
  • Eventually automating engineering tasks.

Sequencing

Sunrun comes later. Sunpool and Sunline are higher priority because Sunrun depends on them — missions need a memory layer to read from and a task layer to schedule into.

Case A

Manual Claude commit.

A team member opens Claude (or another MCP client) and edits a project's MD file directly. The change appears in Feed as a proposed commit, gets approved, and writes back to the source of truth.

Trigger

Team member edits sunspoke/design.md via Claude.

Steps

  1. Edit submitted via Claude.
  2. Feed surfaces the proposed change with a diff.
  3. Reviewer approves (bulk or granular).
  4. Change commits to the MD file.
  5. Downstream consumers (Paper, Sunline, etc.) see the new state.
Case B

Meeting transcript → updates.

A meeting transcript (this meeting is the canonical example) is ingested. The system proposes requirement updates derived from the transcript. The team approves through Feed.

Trigger

Transcript uploaded (GPT 5.5 Thinking + Assembly AI Transcription pipeline).

Steps

  1. Transcript parsed.
  2. System drafts proposed updates to relevant MD files.
  3. Feed shows transcript-ready notification.
  4. Reviewer approves (bulk or granular diff review).
  5. Approved changes commit to the source of truth.
Case C

Save triggers requirement update.

A save is added to Sunpool and associated with a project. The system proposes an update to that project's MD files based on the save's content.

Trigger

Team texts a competitor reference into Sunpool, tagged Sunspoke.

Steps

  1. Save lands in Sunpool database.
  2. System associates save with Sunspoke.
  3. System drafts a proposed update to sunspoke/design.md (or wherever the save is most relevant).
  4. Feed surfaces the proposal.
  5. Reviewer approves; change commits.
Case D

Sunline weekly tasks.

Sunline reads roadmap.md and generates weekly recommended tasks. The team accepts or rejects each recommendation.

Trigger

Start of week.

Steps

  1. Sunline reads roadmap.md (and other MD files as needed).
  2. Generates a prioritized list of weekly tasks.
  3. Team reviews tasks; accepts or rejects each.
  4. Accepted tasks become tracked work for the week.
  5. Completed tasks feed back into roadmap status.
Case E

Sunrun mission run.

A mission is run from inside a project, Sunpool, or Sunline. Sunrun executes the workflow against the project's source of truth and Sunpool memory.

Examples

  • Find partnerships for Sunspoke.
  • Generate marketing activation ideas for Sunspoke launch.
  • Contact vendors listed in Sunpool tools.
  • Generate a JD for a Sunspell hire.
  • Eventually: automate an engineering task (e.g. open a PR for a roadmap item).

Steps

  1. Mission triggered from a project / Sunpool / Sunline surface.
  2. Sunrun reads relevant MD files and Sunpool saves.
  3. Mission output produced (deliverable, draft, message, code, etc.).
  4. Output routed back to Feed for review where applicable.
Approvals

Approval controls.

Feed must support both bulk approval and advanced granular diff review. Revert / version history is required.

  • Bulk approve — accept a batch of proposed changes in one click.
  • Advanced granular approval — step through proposed changes individually.
  • Diff review — see exactly what is being changed in each MD file before approving.
  • Revert / version history — undo any approved change; see who approved what and when.
  • Transcript-ready notifications — surface in Feed when a transcript is ready for ingestion.

Owner: Jasmine specs the approval flows.

Edges

Quiet failures.

SunOS should be calm when things are missing. Never shaming.

  • Empty Home (no projects) — invite to create the first project; default to Sunspoke during v1.
  • Empty project folder (no MD files yet) — show the file template with prompts to fill.
  • Empty Sunpool — show how to text in the first save.
  • Empty Sunline (no roadmap.md) — explain that tasks are derived from the roadmap; offer to draft one.
  • Empty Feed — quiet, not promotional.
  • Failed transcript ingestion — surface the file and let the team retry.
  • Conflicting proposed changes — surface both, let the reviewer pick.
  • Sunrun mission failure — surface the failure with the relevant MD context, let the team retry.

v1 success is measured by Sunspoke shipping coherently from MD source-of-truth, with the team's tools (Paper, Claude, Feed) all reading from the same substrate.

  • Sunspoke can be specified, designed, and prototyped end-to-end from its MD files.
  • Wayeez can produce the Sunspoke homepage from design.md + product.md in Paper without re-briefing.
  • Michael's Sunpool database accepts texted-in saves and exposes them via API.
  • Centralized MD files are reachable by Claude / MCP clients.
  • The Sunspell GitHub exists and contains the playground work.
  • At least one transcript (this one) lives in the source of truth as a PRD.
  • What we won't optimize for: external usage, multi-project breadth, polished marketing surfaces.
Mood

Tone and references.

References that ground the SunOS mental model.

  • Desktop folders — for Home (project tiles, cover images, last-updated stamps).
  • Pinterest / Are.na — for Sunpool's masonry of saves.
  • GitHub PRs — for Feed's diff + approval pattern.
  • Linear / Asana — for Sunline's task surface (but read out of the roadmap).
  • Notion / Slack Canvas — anti-references. SunOS replaces them as the requirements substrate, not as chat.
  • MCP servers — for the Claude / client connection layer.
